MULTIMEET UI/UX REDESIGN

SCHEDULE OPTIMIZATION & EDITING WEB APP

OVERVIEW

  • Tools: Sketch, Adobe Illustrator, Principle
  • Deliverables: UI/UX, Wireframing, Prototyping, User Testing
MultiMeet is a scheduling platform (currently specific for the theater scene: stage managers and actors) that is able to handle mutually dependent events and prioritize events based on the rules and constraints entered by the user, generating an optimized schedule for all participants involved.

PROBLEM

Currently, scheduling is done by hand by the stage managers where they have to account for the actors’ external availabilities and make their rehearsal schedules as efficient as possible. This task can take a few hours to complete and is very tedious.
MultiMeet tackles this pain point by being able to generate an optimized schedule in minutes, however, users have expressed the desire to be able to edit a schedule manually after the optimized schedule has already been generated.

SOLUTION

I will be coming up with a user friendly design that allows users to be able to manually edit a MultiMeet generated schedule that allows them to view how efficiently everyone’s time is being used in order to make smart adjustments.

MY ROLE

My role in this project was to work with the founder of MultiMeet and redesign the editing feature of the scheduling platform by taking notes on data and feedback they already received from previous beta testing, brainstorming and wireframing potential solutions, and prototyping and testing designs with existing MultiMeet users.

USERS WANTS & NEEDS

Before diving into brainstorming solutions and sketching, I wanted to clarify and confirm what MultiMeet users would want and need while editing their schedules. This is the list of requirements I organized after consulting with MultiMeet's founder:
  • The current schedule
  • A list of rehearsals that are not yet scheduled
  • Information about who is needed for each rehearsal
  • Information about everyone's conflicts/availabilities
  • Ability to visualize the schedule from the perspective of an individual participant
  • Allow different variants of the schedule (e.g. ability to save or revert back to old versions)
  • Basic information of individual rehearsals (length, what is being rehearsed, etc.)
  • Information about who is needed for a particular rehearsal
  • Information about conflicts and availability affecting a particular rehearsal

BRAINSTORMING & LOFI MOCKUPS

Now that I had a better idea of what features were needed and what information needed to be accessible, I came up with three different designs. I presented these designs to the founder to see what he thought. After our meeting where we did a critique of each design and bounced ideas off of each other, the main takeaways I got were that:
  1. He liked that the user would be able to see the current and new schedules side by side as they made edits in design #1.
  2. It’s nice to be able to see the conflicts and availabilities of each participant in a particular day and be able to see how the rehearsal to be rescheduled will affect each participant’s schedules as in design #2. However, he thought it made more sense to switch the axes of the times and actors aka have the times of the day listed vertically down the left side of the table and the actors listed horizontally across the top.

USER TESTING (FIRST ROUND)

About the user:
She has worked as a stage manager on several Shakespeare productions (Hamlet, The Taming of the Shrew, King John) and shares the pain points of having to tediously construct schedules manually using Excel spreadsheets. She is also familiar with the early stages of MultiMeet.

  • Text is really small/hard to read
  • Was not sure if lighter or darker colors were "better" in terms of availability (eventually concluded that darker was better)
  • Immediately deduced that in order to reschedule an event, you could click on it and drag it around (because it's similar to how Google calendar works)
  • Thought the "undo" button would act like a "back" button and bring the user back to the previous page they were on
  • Might be more intuitive if there was only one calendar instead of having a "current" and "new" because user was unsure if they were supposed to reschedule event by dropping it into the current calendar or new calendar
  • Would like to be able to easily switch between days with similar participant availabilities to see how well the event would fit in each day before finalizing the reschedule
  • If there are many people needed for an event, it might be annoying to have to continuously scroll left and right in order to view everyone's availabilities
  • Suggestion: collapse/condense the table by representing each participant by their initials
  • Maybe also include in the tab that states the sum total of conflicts there are as you drag the event up and down along the schedule
  • Would like to be able to click on events under "unscheduled" to see available times in the calendar and drag and drop those events into the calendar as well
  • Interesting idea: ability to select a block of time and be given suggestions for what events could be placed there given participants' availabilities

ITERATE

Video of high fidelity interactive prototype. Helper text added on screens to further clarify what action is expected of the user.



Initial edit mode screen. In retrospect, I could have made the calendar dimensions larger to fill up the extra white space on the screen, which would have also let me increase the text size for better accessibility. I also added helper text on this screen and others to clarify what action the user should perform.

Added more detail about the rehearsal being rescheduled in the rescheduling tab (when, for what, for how long, and who is and isn't available during that time). I also added a handle using a cluster of eight small squares and arrows pointing up and down to really emphasize that the rescheduling tab is something you can drag vertically.

Each rehearsal participant was reduced to just their avatars. If you want to be reminded who the avatar represents, you can hover over it to reveal a tooltip with their names/characters.

You can click on and drag another rehearsal to reschedule.

You can see the rehearsal's availabilities in the calendar above. If you want to reschedule it to a different day, just simply drag it to the day you prefer in the calendar. I've also removed the "Save & Invite" button to avoid confusion because you shouldn't be able to save or invite anyone to an invite before you have finalized your edits.

Clicking on a rehearsal shows its availabilities on the same calendar rather than another calendar side by side and grays out all the other rehearsals. I added a legend that specifies the darker shading to mean more people are available during that time slot and lighter shading to mean fewer people are available since there was confusion.

If you try to click on the rescheduling tab instead of dragging it, the rescheduling tab will shift slightly to the left and up and have a drop shadow to further emphasize that this is an element that is separate from other elements on the screen and can be moved around.

You can also click on the button "Hide Calendar" to hide the calendar and have the schedule of that particular day fill up more of the screen. The "Hide Calendar" button becomes a "Show Calendar" button.

As you can see, the relevant participants of this rehearsal have been shifted to the front of the table, and the particpants who aren't needed for this rehearsal are grayed out.

USER TESTING (SECOND ROUND)

About the user:
She works as a stage manager and is an existing user of MultiMeet. She specifically mentioned wanting an edit feature during the initial beta tests, which was why she was selected for this user test.

  • It would be helpful to have a legend explaining the different shades of color indicating availabilities
  • “Save & Invite” button should be disabled while in the editing mode
  • Separate the “Save” and “Invite” actions
  • Indicate rehearsal’s original time before edit is finalized
  • Don’t show unscheduled rehearsals if they’re not clickable
  • Show that the rescheduling tab is draggable
  • User likes being able to see both the month and specific day schedule at the same time but could make calendar minimizable to allow more space for day

POTENTIAL NEXT STEPS FOR MULTIMEET

  • Increase text size for better overall visibility
  • Add back button for if the user wants to exit out of the edit mode
  • Brainstorm more improved solutions for condensing rehearsal participants

WHAT I LEARNED

This was my first time working on a product for an unfamiliar field! I had to learn about all the little nuances and unique frustrations stage managers had to deal with while trying to create a rehearsal schedule that is suitable for all participants involved in the play. This was a very fun and educational experience for me because I really had to listen to the wants and needs of the users and figure out how to design to tackle their problem since I didn’t have personal experience with stage management to draw from.

I also had the opportunity to work with the founder of the product and learned how to balance what the founder wanted out of the redesign with what the users told me they wanted from the feedback they gave me during user tests.

I also learned a lot about the pros and cons of incorporating a disabled button in the UI. Disabled buttons are generally bad due to its low accessiblity, and they give little to no feedback when a user clicks on it and nothing happens. A disabled button could be useful to indicate that there is more work to be done on the screen and could give an informative error message if a user tries to click on it before completing all necessary actions.

In addition to learning about the design of disabled buttons, I also learned how to add indicators to my design to show that something is draggable by adding handles, arrows, shadows, and shifting the element slightly to show that it is something that can be moved around.

I hope my redesign of the MultiMeet edit feature helps the future design direction of MultiMeet and can’t wait to see how it grows!
